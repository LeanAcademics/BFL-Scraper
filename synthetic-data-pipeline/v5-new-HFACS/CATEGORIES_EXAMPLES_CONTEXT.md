# Context Examples

Following are common examples of codes per different category. Do not treat them as an exhaustive list but as an example for a more thorough understanding of the Categories and possible Labels.

## 002OC: Other Cause

- **Unforeseeable environmental event:** Unsafe conditions that were not identifiable as unsafe before the jump, given what a competent BASE jumper could reasonably have assessed from the exit point. If the conditions were observable or forecastable before the jump and the jumper decided to jump anyway, the decision is a human error (001HE), not an other cause.
- **Equipment failure independent of human error:** A material or mechanical failure of equipment that was not caused by the jumper's packing, rigging, assembly, maintenance, modification, or equipment selection. If the jumper packed incorrectly, misrigged, used inappropriate gear for the jump, or failed to maintain equipment, the equipment failure is a consequence of human error (001HE).
- **Other non-human cause:** Any other cause supported by positive evidence in the narrative where the jumper acted within accepted practice and the fatal outcome resulted from a factor the jumper could not reasonably have anticipated or controlled. Includes sudden medical event, with no prior indication. If the jumper had a known medical condition that made such an event foreseeable and chose to jump regardless, the decision to jump is a human error (001HE), not an other cause.

## 101D: Decision Errors

- **Wrong procedure applied:** The jumper applied an incorrect defined procedure to a recognized situation.
- **Poor judgment or risk acceptance:** The jumper consciously selected an inappropriate course of action in a situation requiring judgment. This includes choosing to jump in conditions that clearly exceed safe limits, selecting an object  way beyond the jumper's current ability, choosing a flight line with insufficient margin, attempting maneuvers without sufficient proficiency or choosing to deploy at a dangerously low altitude.
- **Improvised response to novel situation:** The jumper faced a compound or cascading emergency for which no practiced procedure existed and devised a real-time solution that proved inadequate.

## 102S: Performance/Skill-Based Errors

- **Attention failure:** The jumper failed to monitor or respond to critical information during execution. The information was available but the jumper did not attend to it. Includes losing altitude awareness during freefall and failing to assess terrain proximity during wingsuit flight.
- **Memory failure:** The jumper failed to perform an action they knew to be necessary and intended to perform. The jumper failed to carried out something they would have done if reminded. May include forgetting to connect the pilot chute to the bridle after a repack, leaving a packing tool inside the container, or forgetting a planned deployment altitude. Coded only if the omission was the direct active cause for the fatal outcome.
- **Technique error:** The jumper attempted the correct action but executed it with inadequate technique. May include poor body position at exit or deployment, incorrect packing technique, weak pilot chute throw, and poor tracking or flight technique resulting in insufficient separation.

### 201C1: Mental Awareness Conditions

- **Inattention/Complacency:** Failed to maintain sufficient readiness or situational awareness, including reduced alertness from high familiarity with a site, equipment or procedure that produced an unjustified reduction in alertness.
- **Channelized Attention/Fixation:** Focused all attention on a limited number of cues to the exclusion of other critical information. Typically manifests as fixation on one problem such as body position recovery while failing to monitor another such as altitude.
- **Task Saturation:** Demands exceeded mental capacity in the time available. The jumper was cognitively overloaded, not inattentive. Most relevant during cascading malfunctions or compound emergencies within a window of seconds.
- **Distraction:** An external stimulus diverted attention from the primary task at a critical moment. Such as camera equipment or other jumpers.
- **Geographically Lost:** The jumper was at a different position relative to terrain than they believed. Such as wingsuit pilots flying another line than intended or jumpers who jumped from the wrong exit point.

### 201C2: State of Mind Conditions

- **Psychological Disorder:** A diagnosable psychological condition that impaired the jumper’s judgment or decision-making capacity.
- **Life Stressors/Emotional State:** External life circumstances such as relationships or stress impaired cognitive performance or judgment on the day.
- **Personality Style:** An enduring behavioral pattern such as arrogance or perceived invulnerability that contributed to the unsafe act. Requires strong narrative evidence of a stable enduring pattern, not a single-instance inference.
- **Overconfidence:** The jumper unreasonably overvalued or overestimated their own capability relative to the specific task. Requires strong narrative evidence that the jumper acted in a manner inconsistent with a reasonable jumper (their overestimation is above and beyond what a reasonable jumper in a similar situation would have been expected to do), do not infer from the outcome alone.
- **Pressure/Haste/Misplaced Motivation:** An identifiable pressure or motivation, separate from the merits of the decision itself, drove the jumper to proceed. The jumpers motivation to complete a task was misplaced, and/or they knowingly pressed themselves beyond reasonable capabilities which resulted in a hazardous condition or unsafe act. Requires strong narrative evidence that the jumper felt compelled to proceed.

### 201C3: Adverse Physiological Conditions

- **Substance Effects:** Performance impaired by alcohol, drugs, or medication.
- **Physical Illness/Injury:** A pre-existing illness or injury degraded performance while not directly producing the fatal outcome.
- **Physical Overexertion:** Acute physical overexertion immediately before or during the jump degraded performance such as exhaustion from a strenuous approach hike or from performing many jumps in a single day. Distinct from Fatigue, which is general and sleep-related.
- **Fatigue:** General fatigue from sleep deprivation that negatively affected physical or mental performance.
- **Anthropometric/Biomechanical Limitations:** Physical characteristics such as size or strength were insufficient for the specific demands of the task.
- **Dehydration/Poor Nutrition:** Nutritional state or hydration level negatively affected performance.
- **Startle Response/Freeze:** An automatic physiological response triggered by an unexpected event, resulting in temporary inability to act.
- **Spatial Disorientation:** Sensory systems failed to correctly perceive position, motion, or orientation relative to terrain. Includes visual illusions such as flat light, snow cover, leafless vegetation and featureless terrain that caused the jumper to misperceive altitude, distance, or terrain proximity,  and vestibular disorientation leading to loss of spatial orientation due to tumble, spin, or in visually impoverished conditions the caused the jumper to misperceive their body position, heading, or proximity to the object where they attempted to act based on a false sense of orientation.

### 202P1: Jump Preparation and Communication

- **Failure to perform a pre-jump gear check:** Failure to perform or have someone else perform a pre-jump gear check in the generally accepted extent that would have caught the L1 factor.
- **Inadequate equipment configuration:** The jumper selected and set up the wrong equipment configuration for the jump and this contributed to the L1 error without being the causal factor itself.
- **Inadequate briefing or coordination:** Insufficient coordination and briefing in group jumps that lead to a misunderstanding or misalignment contributing to an active failure such as collision during the jump or insufficient separation.

### 202P2: Failure to Correct Known Deficiency

- **Failure to correct a recurring skill issue:** The jumper was aware of a skill/performance deficiency through personal experience, near-misses, or feedback from others and failed to take corrective actions such as additional training, returning to skydiving to practice or adjusting behavior. Includes problems such as a sustained pattern of instability on exit, stalls on wingsuit openings or tears in the canopy.
- **Allowing for progressively riskier behavior:** The jumper was showing a sustained pattern of action becoming increasingly dangerous heavily exceeding accepted community norms and was aware of it through near misses or other ways.
- **Failure to correct an equipment problem:** The jumper was consciously aware of a sustained deficiency of their equipment or its state and failed to correct it over an extended period of time.

## 203T: Training Conditions (203T)

- **Insufficient Skydiving Experience:** The jumper attempted BASE jumping or advanced disciplines in BASE jumping with clearly insufficient experience from Skydiving. May include things like BASE jumping with less than 150 skydives or wingsuit BASE jumping with insufficient wingsuit skydives or using a new piece of equipment without first getting accustomed to them in Skydiving.
- **Insufficient BASE Experience:** The jumper performed advanced BASE jumps without the commonly required BASE jumping experience. May include wingsuit BASE with little slick and/or tracksuit BASE jumps, or attempting specific jumps from advanced exits without first training the them on an easier exits.
- **Lack of BASE specific training:** The jumper attempted BASE jumping or advanced disciplines in BASE jumping generally accepted to require specific training by themselves instead of taking a First Jump Course, training from a competent mentor or coach.
- **Lack of Currency:** The jumper was previously trained to proficiency but had not performed the relevant task in a long period. May include not BASE jumping for years and attempting advanced jumps or attempting an advanced jump on a suit not used for a long time.
- **Insufficient Knowledge:** The narrative positively establishes that the specific decision reflects a knowledge gap, that accepted BASE education would have corrected. Do not infer from the decision being wrong that the jumper must have lacked knowledge

## (204E) – Environmental Conditions

- **Exit surface condition:** A physical condition of the exit point (snow, ice, wet rock, loose debris, vegetation) degraded the jumper's ability to execute a stable launch and contributed to the L1 error. The condition must be identified in the narrative and contributed to the skill-based-error of exit stability.
- **In-flight atmospheric disturbance:** An atmospheric event during the jump (turbulence, rotor, localized gust, thermal activity) degraded the jumper's ability to maintain body position, heading, or canopy control and contributed to the L1 error. Code only if the event was not reasonably observable or forecastable before the jump.
- **Degraded visual conditions:** Environmental visual conditions (flat light, fog patches, sun glare, darkness, low-contrast terrain) degraded the jumper's ability to assess terrain proximity, or maintain orientation, contributing to the L1 error.
- **Ambient temperature effect:** Ambient cold or heat physically degraded the jumper's execution capacity (numbed hands affecting handle grip, heat-induced fatigue affecting coordination) and contributed to the L1 error.
