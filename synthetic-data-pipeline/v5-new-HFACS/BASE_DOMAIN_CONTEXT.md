# Before You Begin

BASE jumping is the practice of parachuting from fixed objects. B.A.S.E. stands for Building, Antenna, Span, and Earth. BASE Numbers are awarded to those who have completed at least one jump from each of the four objects.

Every BASE jump carries a risk of death. Unlike skydiving, which provides a reserve parachute, an automatic activation device (AAD), and thousands of feet of reaction time, BASE jumping uses a single parachute system with minimal altitude margin.

# Pre-BASE Training

## Skydiving

The accepted learning path: Complete Skydive AFF → 200+ skydives → Find a mentor and First Jump Course (FJC) → Complete FJC → Begin mentorship → Jump simple objects with experienced jumpers → Progress to less simple objects.

The generally accepted minimum is 200–250 skydives before beginning BASE. Some jumpers with strong balance/spatial awareness and athletic backgrounds may be adequate at 200; most require more. Time in the sport is as important as jump numbers. Rushed progression is a recognized risk factor.

Skydiving teaches the core skill set for BASE: freefall body control and canopy piloting. The canopy skills break into three areas: opening, flight, and landing.

## Opening

Heading performance is critical in BASE. Training begins with awareness of opening headings during skydiving.

### Body Position at Pull Time

A stable, balanced body position at deployment is a key ingredient for on-heading openings. Accepted practice requires achieving a stable position from any freefall attitude and deploying without turning, drifting, or unbalancing the shoulders. Allowing one shoulder to drop even an inch during a BASE opening can negatively affect canopy heading.

### Body Position During Line Stretch / Canopy Inflation

During line stretch and inflation, the jumper must keep the body balanced and responsive, controlling the parachute by weight-shifting in the harness to maintain heading. Head up, eyes on canopy, while maintaining airspace awareness.

### Instant Riser / Brake Control

At line stretch, accepted practice is to reach for rear risers in the moment between line stretch and canopy inflation, without reaching too soon (which compromises body position). By the time the canopy begins pulling the jumper upright, hands should be on rear risers ready to correct heading. Heading awareness must be maintained throughout the opening sequence.

## Canopy Flight

After opening, practice object avoidance drills: fly directly backwards with rear risers and with brakes; make immediate 90° and 180° turns with risers and brakes (riser maneuvers should be practiced with brakes stowed). These drills develop the skills needed to avoid object strike, a leading cause of injury in BASE.

## Landing

BASE landing zones are much smaller than skydive DZs — some smaller than a DZ's packing area, often with obstacles on multiple sides. Accepted practice requires landing consistently on a chosen spot in all conditions. BASE canopies (large, slow, 7-cell) can be "sunk" into tight landing zones more effectively than typical skydiving 9-cells. Skydiving the BASE canopy before BASE jumping it is strongly recommended. Practice in zero-wind conditions is essential, as BASE rarely offers ideal headwind landings.

## Tracking

Tracking is mandatory for terminal objects. An ideal BASE tracking position is steeper and more aggressive than a typical skydive track — an all-out race away from the cliff, maximizing glide ratio rather than minimizing sink rate.

### Tracking Suits

Tracking suits greatly increase forward speed and decrease sink rate, providing significantly increased safety margin through object separation. Their use is considered near-mandatory for terminal cliff objects. Accepted practice requires mastering the tracking suit in skydiving before any BASE use. Minimum of 40–50 tracking suit skydives (in addition to 150–200 original skydives) before BASE jumping with a tracking suit.

Before BASE jumping with a tracking suit: the jumper must have found the ideal body position for maximum forward speed and glide, must have consistently on-heading openings, and must be able to instantly transform any unstable position into a solid track on a predetermined heading.

## Skydiving Your BASE Canopy

Becoming familiar with BASE gear in the skydiving environment is an important and commonly underestimated progression step. Getting BASE gear ahead of the BASE course and learning to assemble, pack, and configure it for different applications is recommended.

Many BASE jumpers have poor canopy piloting because they never skydive their BASE canopy. A 500' bridge teaches far less about a canopy than a 4,000' hop-and-pop. Canopy skills save lives in BASE.

A 7-cell low aspect ratio BASE canopy typically has a longer control range and slower stall speed than a skydiving main. While skydiving the BASE canopy, practice:

- Stall characteristics
- Flying backwards using risers and brakes
- Heading corrections with brakes stowed
- Landing on rear risers (dropped-toggle scenario)
- Front riser accuracy approaches
- Steep accuracy approaches in deep brakes
- Landing from quarter, half, three-quarter brake approaches
- Rapid heading corrections using brakes and using risers (brakes stowed)
- Line twist recovery
- Precision landing (on a specific point)
- Deep Brake Setting (DBS) tuning with sufficient altitude

### Rigging Guidelines for Skydiving BASE Canopy

1. Use acceptable risers — many 3-ring risers designed for BASE are NOT suitable for skydiving.
2. Use appropriate slider configuration for higher-airspeed openings (may require a high-airspeed slider from manufacturer and slider control techniques).
3. Learn proper packing method (free-pack with tail pocket, or deployment bag).
4. Choose appropriate PC for the delay, canopy size, and packing method. The BASE canopy flies differently depending on whether towing a collapsed skydiving PC, a ~32" non-collapsible BASE PC, or a 42"+ BASE PC.
5. Stick to hop-and-pops (not terminal openings). Practice in zero-wind conditions.

## Mentorship and Progression Norms

No FJC in the world provides sufficient education to jump a variety of sites safely or even one site in varied conditions. Continued guidance from experienced mentors is mandatory. The knowledge needed to progress beyond first BASE jumps exceeds what any course can contain.

Not all experienced jumpers make good mentors. A mentor who is disorganized, forgets important details, or fails to present information sequentially is inadequate.

Not jumping is always an acceptable outcome. If stress or fear is present at the exit point, the jumper may be exceeding their readiness.

# Objects

## Evaluating an Object

Four categories govern jump planning:

1. **Terrain:** Exit point, landing area, surrounding terrain, and anything affecting the jumper during freefall, under canopy, or influencing wind.
2. **Weather:** Macro and micro meteorology. Blue skies do not guarantee good jumping conditions.
3. **The Jumper:** Current mindset, skill set, actual ability, mental and physical fitness. Currency is critical — BASE is never like riding a bike.
4. **Legality:** Legal status adds stress and complication as a factor affecting the jumper.

## Buildings

Buildings are technical objects in both logistics and execution. Urban environments present complex wind patterns affected by surrounding structures. Jumping in urban environments requires experienced jumpers with detailed understanding of urban wind effects and refined canopy skills for tight landing areas.

## Antennas

Antennas range from below-jumpable to 2000+ feet. Most large antennas have guy-wires, which restrict desirable heading range. A three-guy-wire antenna provides only ~120° of possible heading range (vs ~180° for buildings or cliffs). A freestanding antenna provides >180° of possible range.

Wind evaluation is mandatory; wind conditions frequently rule out safe jump plans.

**AM antennas** are dangerous when transmitting — the entire structure radiates. Climbing while transmitting risks electrocution and radiation exposure. Generally considered not worth the additional risk.

**FM antennas** carry transmission devices only at upper portions. The structure itself is not hazardous in the same way as AM, but standing near transmitters at close range exposes the jumper to high radiation. Identifying AM vs FM structures requires specific knowledge; uneducated assumptions are unsafe.

## Spans

Generally considered the safest and least technical of objects. Spans afford increased airspace and less object to strike. However, even the simplest spans have seen multiple fatalities. A bridge with a constricted or complicated landing area can be more difficult than many cliffs.

Novice jumpers benefit from repeating jumps on a simple span to refine exits, deployments, landings, and packing while minimizing variables. Introducing one new factor at a time is key to progression.

## Earth (Cliffs)

Cliffs are broadly categorized as slider-down and slider-up, with overlap. Slider-up cliffs divide into terminal and sub-terminal:

- **Slider-down cliffs:** Short delays provide little time for deployment but also less time for errors.
- **Terminal slider-up cliffs:** Provide time to track away at near-terminal/terminal airspeeds. Also provide more time for serious errors.
- **Sub-terminal slider-up cliffs:** Generally considered the most complicated — inadequate altitude for effective tracking but enough time between exit and deployment to make mistakes.

Object avoidance skills should be developed at spans before progressing to cliffs. No BASE object has enough delay to absorb a truly unstable exit.

Off-heading opening with resulting object strike is a known failure mode. Even a "big" object with 10 seconds to impact, good push, and landing directly beneath the exit allows only ~7 seconds of delay. Within 7 seconds, terminal airspeed is not reached; all separation comes from exit push and sub-terminal track.

### Evaluating Cliff Safety

Bigger does not always equal safer. Multiple factors must be weighed:

- **Approach time and exertion:** A 3.5-hour approach means arriving with less energy than a one-hour approach. Physical fitness directly affects performance.
- **Terrain below the cliff:** A large talus slope with rockfall is a more serious forced-landing environment than trees next to a road on a shorter jump.
- **Stress factors:** Additional stressors (legality, unfamiliar site, complex access) compound risk.

When choosing slider-up cliffs early in a career, base decisions on more than just vertical relief. Begin with easy access, alternate landing zones, forgiving terrain, and possibility of quick rescue.

**Sub-terminal tracking is mandatory for slider-up cliffs.** Learning sub-terminal tracking requires time, preferably from objects such as balloons or paragliders.

### Heading Performance and Slider

Heading performance decreases with the addition of a slider, and also at slower deployment speeds.

### Usable Altitude

Usable altitude is the altitude available for freefall and then landing in the planned landing area — not total height of the object. A 2000' cliff that becomes positive after 500' has a usable altitude of 500'. Packing configuration and delay must be tuned to usable altitude, not total vertical relief.

## Other Objects

Jumpable objects that do not fit into B.A.S.E. categories include dams, cranes, bridge support pillars, and chimneys. A common non-fixed-object jump in Europe is from a tandem paraglider (~23 mph / 38 km/h), providing valuable sub-terminal training.

# BASE Gear

BASE equipment is not subject to aviation authority oversight or certification. Equipment can be manufactured and sold by anyone.

## Canopies

BASE canopies are 7-cell designs evolved from skydiving reserve parachutes. Low aspect ratio (~2:1) provides more predictable openings, better heading performance, low glide performance, and long control range — useful for tight landing zones.

Low aspect ratio = high induced drag (Induced Drag = Cl² / (π·AR·e)). Larger wingtip vortices and top-surface turbulation.

BASE canopies differ from skydiving mains: reinforced at critical areas, fitted with tail pockets for line stowage, and equipped with multiple brake settings. High-strength lines are used due to opening forces.

### Design Features

- **Slats:** Top-surface openings near leading edge center span; allow airflow to top surface at high AoA, reducing stall speed.
- **Float Flaps:** Top-surface openings near trailing edge mid-span; add airflow similarly to slats, reducing stall speed.
- **Staggered Brake Cascades:** Staggered attachment points of upper brake lines to main brake line eliminate the "chunk" of finger-trapped line loops, aiding smooth deployment.
- **Engineered Crossports:** Complex patterns allowing improved air transfer during opening.

### Choosing Canopy Size

Accepted mid-range wingloading for BASE: **0.70 lbs/sqft**. Range: 0.60–0.80, chosen based on object type, landing area, elevation, and canopy skill confidence.

A larger canopy is not always easier or safer — lower wingloading reduces headwind performance and responsiveness, which are critical for reaching landing areas and avoiding objects.

At landing altitudes above 5,000–6,000', consider lower wingloading. At sea level with lower density altitudes, more typical wingloading is appropriate.

Wing loading = suspended weight (jumper + all equipment) / canopy surface area (Span × Chord).

### Canopy Evaluation Characteristics

- **Handling:** Progressive and responsive brake travel; predictable and progressive reaction to inputs; stall point characteristics.
- **Heading Performance:** Revealed only over many jumps.
- **Landing:** Deep brake and flare characteristics. A canopy with poor flare can turn a downwind run into a tumble, and a deep brake approach into a stall.
- **Packing:** Thinner, lighter material packs easier.

### Lightweight Canopies

Ultra-light 0-3cfm cloth (PN9 by Porcher Sport) enables systems at roughly half the weight of standard equipment. Lightweight canopies are easier to pack, fit in slimmer containers, and exhibit better opening characteristics (until porosity increases at end of lifespan).

Lightweight cloth is less durable than standard cloth, more susceptible to degradation from water and dirt exposure. As porosity increases over time, glide, speed, and flare performance degrade. Not recommended for hostile desert, sandy environments, or frequent water landings.

## Pilot Chutes

PC manufacturing quality, materials, and precision are all critical. An improperly manufactured PC may have imperfections that reduce heading performance.

Most BASE PCs are built from two flat fabric circles sewn together, creating a deformation-prone shape when inflated. This traditional design causes the inflated PC to be highly prone to asymmetry and deformation. Air spills from the perimeter at random, causing oscillation, spin, or drift — moving the packjob during extraction. Unpredictable PC performance is a root cause of many unexplained off-heading openings.

**Venting** on the top surface increases stability and is important for delays >1–2 seconds.

### PC/Bridle Entanglement Malfunction

One of the most complex and serious malfunctions in BASE. Occurs when the PC tumbles in the jumper's burble and entangles with the bridle to the point of rendering the PC useless — can result in total malfunction and impact.

Two main causes: (1) improperly deployed PC (weak toss, holding PC too long allowing bridle to flow downstream), and (2) heavy PC design (bridle blown past the PC in relative wind, PC collides with it). Heavy handles encourage entanglement.

Solution: Do not hold the PC in hand; throw it into clean air.

### PC Guidelines

- Handles should be very lightweight. Heavy or "grippy" handles contribute to PC/bridle entanglements.
- Toroidal arc designs are more stable, enabling better heading performance, particularly at higher airspeeds.
- Center-top vents and ring vents increase PC stability (at cost of drag force).
- An asymmetric or oscillating PC drifts and pulls the packjob to one side regardless of body position or conditions.
- The PC is the first link in the deployment chain; if it malfunctions, every subsequent stage is affected.

### Toroidal Arc PC

3D-shaped ultralight PCs in widespread use since 2014. Significantly more stable than traditional double-disc PCs (supported by wind tunnel testing). The design was open-sourced in 2014.

### PC Material and Design Factors

- **ZP (Zero Porosity):** Higher initial snatch force, quicker inflation. Generally recommended material.
- **F111 (0-3cfm):** Semi-porous, slower inflation. Some jumpers prefer for near-terminal/terminal applications. Contraindicated for slider-down jumping.
- **Vented PCs:** Reduce destabilizing air spillage from lower perimeter. More stable than non-vented.
- **Mesh type:** Small-hole mesh delays PC inflation, which is rarely beneficial. Large-hole mesh is preferred. Whatever size PC, it should inflate as fast as possible.
- **Load tape:** Six radial load tapes provide good balance of weight and performance. Even the most symmetrically reinforced double-disc does not perform as well as a toroidal arc design.
- **Handles:** Lighter is better. Heavy PVC, thick plastic, or "grippy" handles contribute to entanglement. Internal handles offer more substance with the benefit of not being on the external surface where they could touch the bridle.
- **PC Attachment Points:** Single-point attachments are simpler and safer. Failure to properly attach any PC will likely result in death.

### Checking PC and Load Tape Symmetry

Wrap the bridle around your foot and pull opposite load tapes with each hand at the mesh/fabric border. All opposing load tapes should be exactly equal length. If asymmetric, reposition the bridle attachment or contact manufacturer.

## Bridles

The bridle connects PC to canopy. Standard BASE bridle length: **9 feet** (vs 4–6' in skydiving). This allows the PC to extend beyond the jumper's burble before inflating. The burble is relatively small in the first 2 seconds but grows logarithmically with airspeed.

Longer than ~10 feet is considered unnecessary and potentially dangerous (more bridle = more entanglement potential). Deploying the PC into clean airflow to the side while flying at an AoA with laminar airflow over the back is best practice.

Bridles are container-specific and must not be interchanged between differing container systems. When switching canopies, the bridle (attached to the canopy) must be compatible with the container. Standard specification: flat 1" Type-4 webbing with sewn loops on each end, girth hitched to canopy and PC.

## Container Systems

### Velcro Rigs

Simplest to close correctly. Contraindicated for terminal, wingsuit, and aerobatic jumps due to risk of premature opening at high airspeeds. Also contraindicated when the back of the rig may be disturbed during access (climbing, paraglider harness). Velcro degrades with age and foreign material; holding power must be maintained. Should only be used for low-airspeed slider-down applications. Now rare.

### Dual Pin Rigs

The most popular configuration. More tightly contained than single pin or Velcro. Two pins and routing the bridle is more complicated, but the system is the most versatile, durable, and secure currently in use. Suitable for all applications.

Pin tension should be approximately equal on both pins. Both pins should pull out with a few pounds of pressure (if the packed rig can be lifted by the bridle, something is wrong).

### Pin Lock Malfunction

Pin lock occurs when the bridle loads the pin cover flap at an angle preventing release of the bottom pin. Results in pilot chute in tow. Can be fatal. Easy to create on some rigs still manufactured. Simple solution: jump with pin cover flap open for shorter delays. Understanding the factors that create pin lock on your specific container is essential.

### Floating Pin Bridles

Designed with bridle webbing passing through one pin eye to slide on the bridle. Went out of use after a closed-container impact fatality attributed to a user-modified floating pin bridle. Key lessons: modifying BASE equipment outside manufacturer recommendations is dangerous; container designs with decades of safe use can still have potentially fatal malfunction tendencies.

## Protection

### Helmets

Minimum standard for every jump: a high-quality helmet rated for airsports, motorsports, or alpine skiing/snowboarding. Only CE-rated non-recoverable foam (EPS) helmets provide the brain deceleration rates necessary to help prevent severe head injuries. Skydiving camera helmets provide little to no crash protection. Full-face helmets provide additional face and dental protection.

### Footwear

Supportive footwear with no lace hooks or protrusions that could snag lines. Options range from trail running shoes to above-ankle paragliding boots.

### Body Armor

Protective gear from mountain biking, motorcycling, and snow sports can provide additional protection for landings in technical terrain. Hinged hard-plated armor and closed-cell foam padding exists for virtually every body part. Reactive foam protection (intelligent molecules that lock together on impact) is also available.

Good canopy skills are more effective than body armor. First line of defense is judgment and training. Armor is a supplementary measure for objects where unplanned off-headings might result in landing in undesirable locations or object strike.

# The Deployment Process

## Links in a Chain

The equipment forms a chain from pilot chute to leg straps in which every link is vital. Understanding the deployment sequence is critical.

### The Pilot Chute

The PC must be deployed in a clean and balanced manner with force. A weak PC toss can cause PC hesitation (the PC struggling to inflate in the burble). It can also encourage bridle-PC or bridle-jumper entanglements, particularly during unstable or aerobatic deployments. Overthrowing can cause the PC to oscillate, rotate, and lead to asymmetric canopy extraction and off-heading openings.

### Bridle Extension to Packjob Extraction

The PC generates snatch force to extract the packjob from the container. PC size is critical:

- **Too large:** Causes excessive center cell strip, deforming the packjob asymmetrically, degrading heading performance.
- **Too small:** Increases deployment timeline, causing altitude loss and more time for packjob rotation before line stretch.

Most off-headings are decided before line stretch by rotation of the packjob. Causes of packjob rotation:
- Unstable/oscillating pilot chute
- Container wall influence from bad body position
- Asymmetric or imbalanced packjob
- Asymmetric center cell folding
- Wind influence on low-airspeed deployments
- Center cell strip from oversized PC

### Bottom Surface / Vents

After extraction and line extension, bottom surface expansion occurs before canopy inflation. Airflow on the bottom surface encourages expansion along span and chord. A vented canopy inflates faster via bottom surface vents before the nose fully opens.

### Slider Descent

For slider-up openings, slider descent begins as the last lines release from the tail pocket and bottom surface expansion begins.

- **Large-hole mesh sliders:** Faster descent, slightly more airflow.
- **Small-hole mesh sliders:** Higher drag, slower descent. Useful for canopies that tend to open fast.
- **Sail sliders:** Generally not applicable to BASE due to grossly reduced heading performance (slower slider = more time for heading change).

Adjust airspeed or packing/equipment configuration to achieve comfortable openings.

### Canopy Inflation

The canopy expands to full span and chord dimensions before full pressurization. Until pressurized and developing a wing shape, the canopy has no forward flight characteristics. As pressure builds and the profile takes shape, the canopy transitions from parachutal stall to forward flight with a forward surge.

**For slider-down jumps:** Rapid expansion without slider constriction creates potential for massive forward surge — deep brakes are needed to control it.

**For slider-up:** The slider tempers expansion, reducing surge tendency. The slider effectively deepens the brake setting during early opening phases by forcing control lines through slider grommets.

If the canopy opens in stall (even momentarily) after any jump type, brake settings may need adjustment (or the canopy may need manufacturer inspection). After every jump, the canopy should fly as close to stall as possible with minimal forward speed. Not adjusting brake settings to the slowest possible setting increases the odds of object strike. The difference between properly and improperly adjusted brake settings can be the difference between object strike and a corrected off-heading.

### Forward Flight

After full expansion, pressurization, and forward surge transition, the canopy generates lift and glides forward.

**Wingloading affects DBS:** Different wingloading on the same DBS setting yields different results. Higher wingloading = more tendency to surge at a given brake setting. Lower wingloading = more tendency to stall. All wings have their own stall speed and trim speed for each wingloading. DBS = Deep Brake Setting.

## Maintenance and Inspection

In BASE, the jumper alone is responsible for equipment maintenance. There is no DZO, S&TA, or rigger to inspect gear. Equipment ignorance is not acceptable.

# Choosing Partners

## Partner Selection Norms

Who you jump with may determine how long you live. A suitable partner:
- Checks weather forecasts and evaluates conditions over coming days
- Checks with locals about current situation (recent incidents, landing area sensitivities)
- Shows the landing zones and explains local wind patterns
- Ensures appropriate rescue preparedness
- Selects the most forgiving exit available when introducing a less experienced jumper to a new site
- Takes time getting ready at the exit point — explains landing area, suggests delay and landing pattern
- Does not pressure others to rush checklists or jump in marginal conditions

An unsuitable partner:
- Pressures others in marginal conditions without regard for companion's experience
- Pushes a less experienced jumper into mandatory tracking situations at unfamiliar sites
- Shows impatience at the exit point
- Selects objects based on excitement rather than suitability for the group
- Drinks heavily before jumping

Arriving at a new cliff and heading to the highest exit requiring mandatory tracking is not appropriate for a newer jumper. Fatigue, unfamiliar terrain, wind, new site, and peer pressure from a more experienced partner can push risk from almost-acceptable to unacceptable.

## Group Dynamics at the Exit

A partner's visible confidence level can influence others' decisions. Group pressure can sway experienced jumpers into jumping in conditions they would otherwise decline. Voicing concern when conditions are marginal or someone is jumping beyond their ability level is an accepted community norm.

## Instructor and Mentorship Conflicts

Financial relationships between instructors and students can create conflicts. A paying student's progression may be tied to purchases, complicating the relationship. An instructor may have financial interest in bringing a student to sites contraindicated for that student's experience level.

No instructor can impart missing experience in the split second a canopy opens facing the wall. Muscle memory for instant corrections can only develop through training. Without object avoidance drills practiced at safe sites (bridges), a minor off-heading can be catastrophic instead of merely exciting. Heading to a low cliff with low experience is a recognized bad decision.

A more experienced jumper standing on the exit point cannot protect you from yourself. Each jumper is alone responsible for their own performance and survival.

# Emergency Preparedness

Every group must have an emergency plan. Everyone jumping together should know what to do and who to call. Requirements:
- First aid knowledge
- Knowledge of nearest medical care facility
- Working, charged phone (or alternative communication plan)
- Jumping partners have each other's emergency contact information and medical needs
- Solo jumpers must inform a trusted person of location and expected return time

# From Packing to PLF

## Packing

The single parachute must open and function correctly on every jump. While clean and careful packing is mandatory, packing is not the only factor determining opening outcome. A broad estimation: body position accounts for ~80% of opening performance, with the remainder reliant on packing and weather. However, any single abnormal factor (inappropriate weather, outdated equipment, terrible packing) will override all others.

Packing carelessly or jumping in sub-optimal conditions that works 9 out of 10 times, where failure means serious injury or death, is unacceptable odds.

### Packing Environment

- Pack on a comfortable surface free of distractions (soft surface preferred; grass ideal)
- Have all tools close at hand before beginning; do not walk away from a half-finished packjob
- Minimize interruptions — beginning a task and finishing without interruption is an established maxim of parachute rigging
- Consistency in packing produces consistency in performance — if packing is always the same, the packing variable can be removed when investigating irregular results
- Tool count: most jumpers use 2–6 clamps and a pull-up cord; count tools before and after

## The Deep Brake Setting (DBS)

The DBS is the brake setting used for slider-down openings. At DBS, the canopy should fly as close to stall as safely possible after opening and pressurization. This ensures minimum forward speed in the event of an off-heading near an object.

**Tuning DBS:** Repeat jumps at a simple span, gradually deepening the DBS until the correct setting is found. The canopy should fly as slowly as possible without stalling. A too-deep setting causes stall on opening (canopy "rocks back") — releasing brakes is an instant cure. Only allow this to happen once; adjust immediately.

Tuning DBS naturally tunes the shallow brake setting (SBS) as well. Test both settings from a safe object before jumping a less forgiving one.

## Slider Configuration

**Slider off vs. slider down:** Slider off is a cleaner configuration, but removing the slider increases rigging error risk. Leaving the slider on (secured to front risers only) simplifies conversion between slider-up and slider-down configurations. Never attach the slider to rear risers (interferes with rear riser steering).

For slider-up or slider-down, be aware of snag/entanglement risk between risers and helmet (especially helmets with camera systems).

### Control Lines: In or Out for Slider Down

**Outside guide rings (LRM — Line Release Modification):** Allows dropping a toggle to clear a line-over malfunction. Cost: risk of accidentally dropping a toggle.

**Inside guide rings:** Prevents accidental toggle loss. Preferred when the landing area requires full canopy control and toggle loss risk exceeds line-over risk.

The choice depends on a cost-benefit analysis of line-over probability vs. accidental toggle drop probability for the specific site.

## The Tail Gate

A device to encourage nose-first inflation and proper staging of lateral tail expansion during slider-down deployments (during slider-up, slider grommets serve as the reefing device). Prevents tail inversion (the canopy's tail inflating through itself, causing symptoms similar to line-over).

Common forms:
- **Tail gate:** Small braided Dacron line secured to innermost C-line attachment, wrapping around control lines and inner Cs, secured with rubber band.
- **Masking tape:** One or two wraps of 1" paper tape around control lines and inner Cs.

Some form of tail/brake line control is mandatory for slider-down jumps. Many jumpers also apply tail gates for slider-up jumps (line-overs are possible even with a slider). These methods encourage but do not guarantee nose-first inflation.

## Brake Settings: Deep vs. Shallow

Canopies have at least two brake settings: deep (slider-down) and shallow (slider-up). The original single brake setting was developed for terminal slider-up (skydiving) openings.

**Why deep brakes exist for slider-down:** A canopy in deep brakes flies at lower airspeed. In the event of an off-heading, slower forward speed = more time to correct heading = better chance of avoiding object strike. Even small speed reductions provide meaningful additional reaction time.

**Why the deep setting cannot be used for slider-up:** The slider forces brake lines through grommets, increasing brake influence. Combined with the slider restricting expansion, the canopy will stall on opening.

**Forward speed analysis for a 400' cliff (2-second delay, 20' separation, zero wind):**
- In DBS: canopy moves toward wall at ~6 mph → ~3 seconds to impact → sufficient time for a 90° heading correction.
- In shallow brakes: higher forward speed → significantly less time to correct → greatly increased strike probability.

**When shallow brakes ARE appropriate for slider-down:**
- Static line or PCA deployments from very low objects (<200') where heading performance is generally good, canopy rides are <5 seconds, and altitude conservation for brake-unstow surge and flare is the priority.
- Objects where horizontal separation eliminates wall-strike risk (spans, downwind side of antennas clear of wires, very overhung cliffs).
- When specific wingloading/canopy characteristics produce adequate slow speed from the shallow setting.

**The fundamental principle:** Choose a brake stow position that gives appropriate forward speed for the application. The appropriate setting creates a forward speed that allows enough time to turn the parachute 90° in the event of an off-heading. This is independent of which setting (deep/shallow) achieves it.

**Common myths refuted:**
1. "Unstowing from DBS causes surge into the object" — Only true if you unstow and raise hands to full drive. Unstowing and immediately applying control input prevents surge.
2. "Canopies don't turn fast in DBS" — Slower airspeed means slower turn completion but wider radius = turning further from the object. More altitude consumed, but better than striking the object.
3. "Canopies open faster in shallow brakes" — Canopies surge to faster forward speed sooner, but do not necessarily consume less altitude during opening.

## Pilot Chute Preparation

How the PC is packed affects inflation speed more than the PC model chosen.

- Avoid tools that restrict the PC — forgetting a tool that restricts the PC during packing causes total malfunction.
- Extend the PC's center line to pack it closer to inflated configuration.
- Keep the bridle neatly folded in center, fabric on outside.
- For delays <2 seconds, do not wrap fabric too tightly (delays inflation). Consider going hand-held.
- Research best practices for wingsuit applications, including techniques staging bridle deployment: PC → Bridle → Pins.

## Planning Delay and Deployment Speed

BASE openings are configured to deploy rapidly, taking the jumper from freefall to canopy flight in the shortest time possible. Very hard openings should not be considered normal. Equipment can be damaged or the jumper injured/killed by improper configuration.

**Four critical factors for safe opening speed:**

1. **Control airspeed:** All canopies have a maximum airspeed for each configuration. Excessive delay (excessive airspeed) slider-up or slider-down can result in equipment failure.
2. **Choose the right PC:** Appropriate for the exact delay. "This PC is for tracking" is too vague.
3. **Pack properly:** Correct slider configuration, correct slider type for intended delay/airspeed, correct slider-control method.
4. **Control body position:** Deploying head-down, on back, or unstable can load equipment unnaturally/asymmetrically, potentially causing gear failure.

Equipment Working Load Limit considerations (WLL = MBS / safety factor): Some BASE equipment components are vulnerable with a 10:1 safety factor — certain integrated riser designs, some 3-ring risers, certain Dacron line strengths on lightweight canopies, and some harness junctions including chest straps. Very hard openings must be avoided.

## Pilot Chute Freefall Chart

| Seconds | Slider Up | Slider Down | Remarks |
|---------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| 0-1 | NO | Yes 46" | 42" PC possible in many cases |
| 2 | Not Recommended | Yes 42-46" | If SU: heading performance may be very poor; improper packing may create dangerous snivel |
| 3 | Maybe, not recommended* | Yes 42" | SU only if heading not a concern and adequate altitude for slower opening |
| 4 | Maybe* | Yes 38-42" | SD will be hard. SU slow, only if heading not a concern and adequate altitude |
| 5-7 | Yes 34-38" | NO | SU heading performance improving but not optimal |
| 7-8+ | Yes 32-36" | NO | Beginning of optimal heading performance for SU |
| 8+ Tracking Suit | 32-38" | NO | Tracking suits reduce descent rates and increase burble size |
| 8+ Wingsuit | 36-42" | NO | Wingsuits yield extremely low descent rates and large burbles. Up to 38"+ may be needed |

*3-4 second window: slider up or down may be possible. Low-airspeed (<5 second) slider-up openings yield the least predictable heading. Above 4 seconds, slider-down openings are potentially dangerous to jumper and equipment.

**PC sizing principles:**
- Heavier canopy + lower airspeed = more PC drag required.
- Lighter canopy + higher airspeed = less PC drag required.
- Too large: causes center cell strip (packjob deformation), out-of-sequence deployments, premature slider descent, line dump — can damage jumper and equipment.
- Too small: increases deployment timeline, altitude consumption, and time for packjob rotation.
- BASE PC sizes tend toward slightly oversized for caution, but oversized PCs cause real problems.

## Hand Held vs. Stowed

**Hand held:** Exiting with PC already in hand. **Stowed:** PC packed into BOC pocket.

Going hand-held is recommended for delays <2 seconds. The risks of stowed deployment for <2 second delays: very small time window to locate and deploy PC, and altitude for PC to go from stored state to fully inflated is limited.

A stowed PC must transition from maximally compressed to fully inflated and functioning. At very low airspeed (<2 seconds), this is optimistic. Many jumpers intending a 2-second delay actually take 0.75 seconds.

Go stowed when:
- Exit position prevents comfortable hand-held preparation
- Performing aerials

In most other circumstances for <2 second delays, hand-held is generally safer.

## Burbles

Behind every jumper in freefall is wake turbulence (the burble). A weakly or improperly thrown PC can be entrained in the burble, rendering it unable to extract the canopy. The PC must be deployed into clean airflow to the side. A proper hand-held deployment sees the PC already extracted and released into clean air with an overhand motion.

## Inspecting and Maintaining Equipment

Packing provides an opportunity to inspect before each use:

- Brake stow loop stitching, knot, and Velcro: free of wear/damage
- Lines: re-line per manufacturer recommendations; lower control lines may need earlier replacement
- Soft links or steel maillons: closed 1/4 turn past finger tight
- Riser/harness attachment points: inspect before each use
- Line finger-trap bar tacks: fully intact, no slippage
- Slider bumpers: must fully cover steel links (exposed links nick grommets, damaging lines)
- Line attachment points at canopy: all intact, bar tacks perfect
- Top/bottom surface and rib seams: check for pulled thread or unraveling
- Bridle attachment point: check stitching and reinforcements
- Bridle bar tacks: no wear signs
- Pilot chutes: never drag or step on; flat-dry in shade if wet; inspect for symmetry and trim; retire/repair if worn or asymmetric
- Container: closing loops replaced when worn; all harness stitching intact; webbing/tape free of abrasion
- Keep gear clean; avoid scooping dirt into leading edge; release trapped insects without crushing

**Line stowing:** Inspect tail pocket Velcro after each jump. Lines should pay out smoothly through bottom channel without forcing Velcro open. Evidence of line dump (Velcro forced open) indicates stowage technique problems. Lines stowed carefully, under moderate tension, with infinity-shaped loops sequenced largest to smallest, stacked progressively like a neatly fanned deck of cards.

**Wet gear:** Never jump wet equipment. If wet, dry slowly in shade. If exposed to salt water, rinse in fresh water first. Never pack wet.

## Exiting

A good jump begins with a good exit. Bad exits usually result in bad jumps, often contributing to bad body position at deployment.

**Beginner exits** (bridge, slider-down, short delay): Head high, pushing up as much or more than out. Target: 45–60° up and out. Overly head-high is equally undesirable. Goal: settle into a stable (not continuing rotation) position for deployment.

**Running exits:** If possible, run off the exit. Running exits provide significant separation. First running exit should not be at a highly technical jump.

**If in a poor exit position:** Priority order: (1) deploy, (2) deploy at the right altitude, (3) deploy in stable body position. Deploying low on your back with an off-heading is bad, but beats impact with nothing out. Whatever happens, pull.

**Exit progression:** Begin with PCA, slowly progress to longer delays from a simple span. First slider-up/terminal jump should be from an object large and overhanging enough to allow recovery from a poor exit.

## Off-Heading Corrections

At a simple span, practice instant heading corrections after every opening. Heading corrections should be done with rear riser or instant brake input. Heading control is the first priority after opening.

### Using Risers

Rear riser turns provide fast, powerful heading change. As the canopy opens, hands should move swiftly to rear risers — by the time the jumper is being pulled upright, hands should be ready to give input. Do not compromise opening performance by reaching too early or violently.

Technique for 180° correction: Input both rear risers to halt forward speed, then release one while maintaining/increasing input on the other. Results in fast, controlled turn.

- Target: 180° rear riser turn in ~1 second
- Aggressive riser input near stall will fully stall the canopy (reducing forward speed further — more desirable than object strike)
- Risers are easier to grab than brakes (larger target area)
- Riser turns keep brakes stowed (minimum forward speed even if a riser is accidentally released)

### Using Brakes

Brake turns are more altitude-efficient than riser turns (less altitude per degree of heading change) and can be faster and cleaner. However, missing the toggle or accidentally releasing it creates serious problems.

Technique for 180° brake correction: Lightning-fast double toggle grab, pull to near-stall, full release on one side while maintaining full input on the other.

- Brake turns use less altitude than riser turns
- Brake turns can be faster than riser turns
- Toggles should be big, bright, and stiffened for easy grabbing

**The bottom line:** Do something. Risers or brakes — correct heading and fly away from the object.

## Off-Headings

Every BASE jumper will experience off-headings. Contributing factors include packing, body position, weather, PC stability, and equipment configuration. If any single factor is too far outside the norm, it overrides all others.

An off-heading far from the object is mainly a landing concern. Near the object, it is life-threatening. Object separation and instant heading correction should always be on the jumper's mind.

## Line Twists

Line twists combined with off-heading are serious. If high enough and not heading toward an obstacle, there may be time to untwist and unstow brakes. If not: **heading first, malfunction second.** Reach or climb above the twists to grab a rear line group or brake line to control heading. Pulling on rear risers when twists are well above them is usually ineffective.

## Malfunctions

### Line-Over

Occurs when a line (usually upper brake line) crosses over the top surface of the inflated canopy. Caused by the wingtip inflating through a rear line group during deployment.

**Causes:** Lack of tail gate use; improperly stowing stabilizer; tail-first inflation; lines not properly packed into center of packjob; poor/inadequate tail reefing.

**Corrections:** Line-overs often self-clear (increased internal pressure forces the line off). If not self-clearing:
- **Slider-down with LRM:** Cycle brakes once if altitude permits. If that fails, release the offending toggle — the free line may slide off the surface. Land with rear risers or one riser and one brake.
- **Slider-up:** Cut the line (hook knife) or release the toggle (if releasable toggles installed). Only solution for non-brake-line line-overs is cutting.

**Releasable toggles:** Allow quick toggle release without LRM. Will not help for non-brake-line line-overs. Can be accidentally released.

**Hook knives** are considered mandatory for BASE jumpers.

### Tension Knots

Line tangles occurring during deployment when poorly stowed lines loop through each other. Prevents full canopy expansion, causing asymmetric flight (spiraling turn on affected side). May not be clearable by cycling brakes, full-stalling, or cutting lines (typically occur in upper lines). Causes: poorly stowed lines, twisted control lines, upper brake line junctions.

### Total Malfunctions

No canopy extraction prior to impact. Known causes: packing/rigging errors, PC/bridle entanglement. Deploying head-down and experiencing bridle entanglement with a foot is both a total error and total malfunction.

### Stowing Stabilizers

Packjob must be reduced to encourage nose-first inflation. The tail should be folded so its expansion is delayed and secondary to nose expansion. Stabilizers must be tucked into the reduced folds so they do not inflate early and work between control lines.

## The PLF

The parachute landing fall absorbs impact by sequentially rolling onto the side of the body. Technique: land feet first, knees and ankles pressed tightly together. Roll onto side of calf → side of thigh → buttock → side of back. Entire movement smooth and fluid. Never extend an arm or leg.

## Physical Fitness

Physical fitness correlates with coordination, which correlates with survival. Arriving at an exit point exhausted and debilitated degrades jump performance. Sufficient mental and physical energy must remain for exit, track, reaction to surprises during opening, landing, and return to vehicle. Cardiovascular fitness and strength to carry equipment are essential for objects requiring long approaches.

## Landing Approaches

### Deep Brake Flying

Knowing the stall point and being able to maneuver at the edge of the usable control range is essential. Practice: bring canopy to near-stall without stalling; practice stalling with controlled recoveries; hold "deep stall" / "parachutal" position (near-zero forward airspeed). Know the asymmetric stall (spin) point.

### Spin vs. Spiral

- **Spin:** Yaw-axis rotation from asymmetric stall. One side stalls and flies backward, rotating around the flying side. Sensation: rotating office chair, no extra G-loading. Difficult for a canopy to maintain for more than a few rotations.
- **Spiral:** Roll/yaw axis rotation at increased speed and G-force. Neither side stalled. Usually caused by closure/entanglement of one side (line-over). Can continue indefinitely if uncorrected. Pilot-induced spirals self-correct when input ceases.

### Approach Types

**Figure-8:** S-turns overlapping before straight final glide. Common mistake: not overlapping enough, drifting toward spot until too high for comfortable final glide.

**DBL (Downwind-Base-Final):** Classic skydive pattern. Requires more space and altitude than most BASE LZs offer.

**Straight and Slow:** Deep brakes facing LZ, sink in without turns. Steepest glide path with >50% brake input held to flare. Requires familiarity with altitude consumed transitioning from deep brakes to trim. Common for lower objects and tight LZs.

**Front Risers:** Under-used technique. Effective for altitude loss, accuracy, and powerful flare.

### Object Fixation

Maintain visual contact with the landing spot throughout the approach. If the spot rises on the horizon, landing short; if it falls, landing long. Staring at obstacles to avoid causes impact with those obstacles.

## Airspeed vs. Groundspeed

A canopy flies within an airmass. Airspeed is constant regardless of wind direction — the only detectable difference is visual (groundspeed). If a canopy flies 20 mph, it flies 20 mph upwind or downwind.

| Condition | Airspeed | Groundspeed | Effective Glide Ratio |
|-----------|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Zero wind | 20 mph | 20 mph | 3:1 |
| 20 mph tailwind | 20 mph | 40 mph | 6:1 |
| 10 mph headwind | 20 mph | 10 mph | 1.5:1 |
| 25 mph headwind | 20 mph | -5 mph (backwards) | Negative |

Downwind landings: resist slowing the canopy too early. Groundspeed feels fast but airspeed is the same. Save flare energy for the end — give a powerful, progressive flare at the right moment from full trim speed.

## Slider Rigging Reference

**Slider-down conversion:**
1. Lower slider, fix to front risers only (not rear risers)
2. Re-route brake lines outside slider grommets and outside guide rings
3. Stow brakes using tuned DBS and appropriate technique
4. Apply tail gate (rubber band, masking tape, etc.)

**Slider-up:**
1. Slider drawn to line attachment points, neatly quartered in packjob
2. Route brake lines through slider grommets and guide rings
3. Stow brakes using shallow brake setting
4. Tail gate optional (but increasingly used)

**Slider removed:** If not using the slider, remove it for simplicity. Control line routing determined by jump circumstances.

# Weather for BASE Jumpers

## Wind Effects by Zone

- **Wind at exit:** Causes discomfort and potential instability.
- **Wind at opening altitude:** Affects opening, usually negatively.
- **Wind at landing:** Makes life easier or harder depending on speed and direction.

These three zones may be governed by independent factors. Know the wind speed and direction for all three.

## Weather Information Sources

Weather information is readily available and must be checked before any jump. Aviation weather services provide freezing levels, winds aloft forecasts, and synoptic conditions. Detailed forecasts from services like Meteo Suisse (Switzerland), NOAA (US), and Windy (global app) are accurate in the short term.

**NOAA Winds Aloft:** Available at aviationweather.gov. Provides wind speed, direction, and temperature at altitude bands (3,000', 6,000', 9,000', etc.) for specific time windows.

**NOAA Hourly Weather Graphs:** weather.gov → enter ZIP → "Hourly Weather Forecast." Shows wind speed/direction, cloud cover, precipitation.

## Compass Wind Communication

Wind direction is described by the direction from which it originates (west wind = from 270°). Everyone in the group should know what direction the exit faces. "Up valley" and "down valley" are common directional references at cliff sites.

## Reading the Forecast

Key factors:
- Surface winds and winds aloft
- Existing and approaching fronts
- Anabatic and katabatic winds (including thermal conditions)

## Micro-Meteorology

Common forecasts tell us whether to go BASE jumping. Micro-meteorology tells us what the air is doing on the days we choose to jump. Understanding how air interacts with terrain on a micro scale is key to good decisions at any BASE object.

### Heat Drives Wind

There is no such thing as "no wind." Atmospheric movement is always present and three-dimensional. On a summer afternoon in the Alps, movement is driven by solar heating: sun heats ground → ground heats air → air rises. Individual heated areas combine into massive organized air movement.

### Anabatic Flow

Warm air traveling up sun-heated mountain faces, organizing and growing. Creates updraft detectable at exit points. Source of valley wind — as heated air rises, it creates a void that draws air from down-valley.

### Valley Wind

Distinct from winds aloft ("Meteo wind"). Valley wind is caused by anabatic heating drawing air through valleys, not by atmospheric wind patterns. The valley wind may not be felt on the summit even when it is strong in the valley below.

**Critical:** Just because there is no strong wind at the exit point does not mean the valley below is calm. Valley wind can be encountered during wingsuit flight, under canopy, or in the landing zone.

### Three Weather Zones in Mountains

- **Summit:** Wind driven by macro atmospheric conditions (winds aloft)
- **Face:** Wind on jumpable days driven by thermal heating (anabatic/katabatic)
- **Valley:** Wind driven by heat or large weather patterns (Foehn)

### Valley Wind Interaction with Terrain

1. Wind (including valley wind and anabatic flow) affects glide performance.
2. Wind creates turbulence when interacting with terrain.
3. Reduced glide performance and turbulence change flight trajectory — this is physics and cannot be overcome by skill.
4. When glide performance is reduced near terrain, margin is lost.
5. How much margin existed initially, and atmospheric movement strength/direction, determine whether you fly over or into terrain.

### Failure Mode: Late Afternoon Valley Wind Change

When conditions change throughout the day — sun moves, valley wind shifts, anabatic flow gives way to crosswind — the same line cannot be flown the same way. A jumper may find inadequate altitude to pass terrain even at max-glide (MID speed). If conditions require 2.1 L/D to pass a ridgeline and valley wind reduces effective glide to 1.5, there is no solution. Skill, experience, and courage are meaningless against physics.

### Failure Mode: Reversed Valley Wind Direction

When heavy cloud cover reduces normal valley wind, or storm fallout creates katabatic flow, normal valley wind can reverse. A light down-valley wind at the exit can be significantly stronger along the flight path thousands of feet below. Below-average start combined with stronger-than-anticipated headwinds can reduce effective glide below terrain clearance requirements. Increasing AoA beyond optimal results in stall with no recovery options.

### Meteo Wind and Valley Wind Interaction

Meteo wind can block, encourage, or modify valley flow. Northwest meteo wind into a NW/SE valley on a hot afternoon amplifies valley wind. West meteo wind on summit of an east-facing jump can erase anabatic flow gains, leaving turbulent rotor instead of lift.

### Foehn

Predictable but invisible and dangerous. Caused by low-pressure systems against the southern Alps. Turbulent, generally sinking, can begin or remain confined to mid-valley altitudes without indication on high exit points.

Alpine forecasts consistently predict Foehn arrival to within an hour. Major events: winds >60 mph, violent gusts, direction reversals of 180° in seconds at >40 mph. Common in winter and summer.

Non-local forecasts may not declare Foehn. Strong south winds forecasted for northern-Alps peaks indicate Foehn.

### Anabatic and Katabatic Flow

- **Anabatic:** Warm air rises up sunny slopes, organizing and entraining more heated air. Wind flows up valleys toward heating terrain. Strongest mid-day to late afternoon.
- **Katabatic:** Cool air flows down shaded slopes and valleys. Evening, nighttime, and pre-dawn. Glaciers can generate strong katabatic flow.

Basic forecasting: mornings/evenings/nighttime = katabatic down-slope. Daytime with sun = anabatic up-slope/up-valley. Stronger heating = stronger anabatic. Most common application: predicting wind direction in a distant landing zone.

**Venturi Effect:** Wind velocity increases as flow is constricted between terrain features (narrowing valleys, between buildings). Valley wind that is moderate in wide sections will increase in narrow sections.

### Gust Fronts

Walls of wind caused by massive fallout from thunderheads. Can appear as a border of rain but harbor extreme winds. Even distant (20–30 miles) cumulonimbus clouds can produce gust fronts that reach the jump site.

### Existing and Approaching Fronts

Cold fronts can break temperature inversions and cause rapid wind increases. Low pressure systems can generate afternoon thunderstorms with lightning and gust fronts. Long mountain approaches (5+ hours, 5,000'+ elevation gain) provide ample time for conditions to deteriorate.

## Lapse Rate

| Lapse Rate | Per 1000 ft | Per 100 m |
|---|---|---|
| Dry adiabatic (DALR) | 5.5°F | 1°C |
| Saturation (moist) | 2-3°F | 0.55°C |
| Average | 3.3°F | 0.65°C |
| Superadiabatic | 5.5-15°F | 1-3.42°C |
| Autoconvective | >15°F | >3.42°C |

Practical use: ~5°F per 1,000 ft or 1°C per 100 m provides general estimation. Useful for estimating summit temperature, likelihood of ice on approach trails, and clothing requirements.

## Inversion Layers

Temperature inversions (cooler air near ground, warmer above) occur when warmer airmass moves over cooler without mixing. Caused by consecutive days of light wind, clear skies, and long nights. Strong high pressure encourages development. Can trap dust/pollution near ground.

## Thermals and Thermal-Induced Wind

Variable wind direction at exit points (first from right, then left, varying intensity) often indicates thermal rearrangement, not true wind direction change — sections of air lifting and being replaced. Dust devils indicate lifting air and are dangerous to light aviators.

## Clouds as Indicators

- **Cumulus:** Driven by thermals. Wider than tall = generally good conditions. Taller than wide = trouble, can develop into storms.
- **Cumulonimbus:** Potential gust fronts and heavy precipitation, even from 20-30 miles away.
- **Lenticular:** High altitude winds. If not windy on surface, it could become so rapidly.
- **Cirrus:** High thin clouds. Generally non-threatening unless indicating approaching front.
- **Virga:** Precipitation not reaching ground. Huge sinking air beneath; gust front potential.
- **Mammata:** Potential for heavy rain and accompanying winds.
- **Stratus:** Often indicates approaching fronts; may contain precipitation.

## Mechanical Turbulence

Wind interacting with terrain and objects creates mechanical turbulence on the leeward side. Even in winds as light as 10-15 mph, obstacles (trees, buildings) create turbulence that makes landing conditions poor. Turbulence extends further downwind in stronger winds. BASE canopies resist collapse but can lose altitude in sinking air or experience pitch oscillations reducing flare energy near the ground.

## Measuring and Estimating Wind

An anemometer is inexpensive and pocket-sized. Getting to know the feel of 5, 10, 15, and 20 mph winds is valuable. Different objects tolerate different wind amounts — some are unsafe with even light wind (slider-down cliffs with restricted areas); others are safer in the right wind.

### Beaufort Scale

| # | Description | Speed (mph) | Visual Clues |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Calm | Calm | Smoke rises vertically |
| 1 | Light Air | 1-3 | Smoke drifts, flags barely move |
| 2 | Light Breeze | 4-7 | Wind felt on face, leaves rustle |
| 3 | Gentle Breeze | 8-12 | Leaves/twigs in constant motion, flags extended |
| 4 | Moderate Breeze | 13-18 | Small branches move, dust/paper raised |
| 5 | Fresh Breeze | 19-24 | Large branches/small trees sway |
| 6 | Strong Breeze | 25-31 | Large branches continuous motion, whistling in wires |

Few objects are good in winds >10 mph. No objects are good >20 mph.

# Low Jumps and Specialized Deployments

## Low Jumps: Freefall

"Low" is generally defined as below ~300 feet. The margins are very small. At the 2-second mark, the jumper accelerates through 60 feet per second. A hesitation of even a fraction of a second from pin tension, micro turbulence, fluttery PC inflation, or imperfect throw can be fatal.

Low freefalls should be reserved for the most experienced jumpers using correct equipment at intimately familiar sites.

## Pilot Chute Assist (PCA)

Used by beginners and experts. Eliminates the need for jumper-initiated deployment and provides rapid opening with minimal altitude loss compared to freefall.

PCA is ideal for beginning BASE (commonly the first jump method) and for low objects. Objects in the sub-200 to 300 foot range are frequently jumped using PCA. PCA generally yields good heading performance, but off-headings and malfunctions remain possible. Packing, body position, and weather remain crucial.

**Giving a PCA:**
- Hold the bridle (not just the PC handle — the handle is not designed to extract the canopy)
- Hold the bridle below the PC with one hand, PC with the other
- Determine together how much bridle to hold (less bridle = more freefall before container opening)
- Hold PC/bridle out away from the object (not behind railings or cliff edge)
- Determine together: hold until container opening, canopy extraction, or full line stretch (full line stretch recommended for quickest openings)
- Ensure a comfortable, secure position
- Watch canopy extraction and line extension; don't release too early or hang on too tight
- Never wrap bridle around hand; avoid positions causing line burn

**A BASE jump at sea level is not the same as at 7,000 ft.** Higher altitude: faster landing speeds, more altitude consumed during openings, more altitude lost in turns.

## Static Line Jumping

Used for objects shorter than ~250-300 feet. A short cord attached to the fixed object connects via 80-lb cotton break cord to the bridle. At line stretch, the cord breaks and the canopy inflates normally.

### Configuration

- **Brake settings:** Sometimes possible to use a shallower brake setting for static line (less altitude lost before brake release, less surge after). Consult experienced jumpers familiar with the specific site.
- **Tail gate:** Mandatory for static line (line-overs more frequent in slider-down, and insufficient altitude to correct).
- **Masking tape:** Alternative control line retention. Half or three-quarter inch, two wraps around brake lines and inner Cs.
- **Bridle:** Subject to greater wear from friction at interface points. Some jumpers use a dedicated static line bridle of stronger material (e.g., 7mm cord). Static line bridles can be shorter (~6 feet) since burble is minimal at low speeds.
- **Break cord:** MIL-T-5661, Type I, 1/4 inch. Use proper break cord for every static line jump.
- **PC must never be stowed during static line jump.**

### Anchor

Break cord attached via anchor with smooth, clean attachment point (ensures even loading at expected tension). Never tie directly to the object.

### Wind and Heading

Two factors affect heading: wind and exit location relative to anchor.

- Crosswind pushes canopy downwind after break-cord release, causing a heading deviation.
- **Tailwind:** Increases altitude consumed before canopy begins flying (must overcome wind speed to achieve forward airspeed). Increases forward surge severity. Static line jumping in more than moderate tailwind is not recommended.
- Jumping left/right of anchor stretches canopy to opposite side, causing heading deviation. Jump as close to anchor as possible.

### Body Position

A horizontal body position causes greater body swing and disorientation. Body should remain closer to vertical. A strong launch can increase canopy surge (forward body momentum) and put rig into a "hammock" position leading to off-headings.

**Off-headings on static line:** Rare but possible. If not pointed at object, a few degrees of turn may avoid collision. If pointed directly at object with scarce altitude, pull both rear risers and land the stalled canopy at the object's base.

**Releasing brakes:** Pull down only far enough to free the riser loop, not further. Pulling further brings canopy closer to stall, increasing subsequent forward surge and altitude loss.

### Exit Point Safety

Proper instruction mandatory. The exit point must be organized to prevent bridle/jumper entanglements. Every jumper must have a knowledgeable person verify they are clear of rigging before jumping — trace the bridle by hand from shrivel flap to anchor. Exiting with bridle looped through a leg or under an arm can easily be fatal.

## Multi-Way Jumps

Multi-way BASE jumps are not multi-way skydives. Lack of altitude and time exponentially increases complication and risk. Multi-ways distract from core tasks and add complications to every process.

### Requirements

- **Exit:** Choose and rehearse exit order. Simultaneous exits must be thoroughly dirt-dived.
- **Freefall:** Know your slot; maintain awareness of all jumpers. Responsibility extends to not crowding, cutting off, or colliding with others.
- **Staged openings:** Vertical and horizontal separation needed. Shorter delays for some jumpers may require different packing (e.g., slider-down even when standard is slider-up). Inadequate vertical separation can cause PC deployments into faces, jumper/canopy collisions, and fatal low-pulls.
- **Horizontal separation:** Spacing on exit helps. Murphy's Law: two jumpers opening at same altitude will both have off-headings toward each other.
- **Stick to the plan; don't die for the plan:** If the plan fails, execute plan B. If opening order must be broken to maintain safe altitude, break it.
- **Self-awareness over partner-awareness:** Watching a partner can reduce your own situational awareness, object separation, and body position. Do not sacrifice your own safety for a camera shot.
- **Landing:** Vertical separation from opening should continue to landing. Pre-planning must establish that the landing area accommodates all canopies.

## Unpacked Jumps

Any jump where the canopy is not packed in a closed container.

### Rollover (McConkey)

Canopy hangs beneath the jumper, leading edge facing object. Jumper performs a full front flip over the canopy. Any wrong move or slip can cause jumper/canopy entanglement. Configuration: remove bridle/PC; secure slider down; stow brakes at DBS; flake carefully; dive forward watching canopy; hands on rear risers. Tail gate recommended. Requires instruction and supervision.

### TARD (Totally Awesome Rapid Deployment)

Canopy flaked into pro-pack configuration, held in front. Jumper exits while holding canopy, releases upward. Same canopy configuration as rollover. Tail gate required. Coil lines neatly. Toss canopy upward to avoid entanglement. Requires instruction and supervision.

### Direct Bag

Separate bag containing the parachute, connected to the object or held by an assistant. Lines deploy from bag first, then canopy. Typically faster opening with less altitude loss than PCA. Good heading performance but sensitive to bag positioning — tilting a few degrees can degrade heading; holding upside down can cause a 180°. Requires experienced supervision. Life is in the assistant's hands.

## Night Jumps

Accepted practice: jump the object in daytime first. Familiarity with exit, freefall, and landing approach is required before attempting with reduced visibility. All landing obstacles (power lines, trees, rocks) must be identified prior to the jump.

Depth perception and spatial awareness are impaired. Night jumps should be objects well within the jumper's capability. If using vehicle lights for landing illumination, the vehicle should face into the wind.

## Water Landings

Whenever canopy, water, and an unbuoyed jumper combine, drowning risk exists. Wingsuit water landings are particularly dangerous (swimming in a wingsuit in current is impossible).

Precautions:
- Check water obstacles, depth, temperature, and current
- Personal flotation devices for any intentional water landing and recommended when unintentional water landing is possible
- Never cutaway/release parachute too early (depth perception over water is unreliable)
- Into-wind landing: flare and hold until canopy is in water behind, then cut away
- No-wind/downwind: light flare, hands up, canopy passes over to rest at line stretch in front
- Cut away as soon as safely in water; swim away carefully; canopy is not worth your life
- To recover: retrieve PC first, then pull canopy by trailing edge (pulling by harness fills canopy with water)

## Assessing Danger: Varying Degrees

Every BASE jump is potentially fatal, but risk varies massively between jumps. Evaluate:

- **Access and exit point quality:** Exposure, footing, loose rocks, ice. Poor exit conditions increase risk regardless of object simplicity.
- **Time to correct off-heading:** Including 180° with line twists. Feet from the object or hundreds of feet? Object topography varies — overhanging cliffs may be more forgiving than vertical or positive ones.
- **Obligatory performance:** Is it mandatory to track past a ledge for safe deployment? Mandatory tracking means requiring top performance with no margin for error.
- **Landing area:** Tight/rocky/ringed with trees? A vertical cliff with a nice LZ may be safer than an overhanging cliff with a hazardous LZ.
- **Plan B availability:** Alternate landing areas for off-headings? If any off-heading means trees/rocks/power lines, recognize that risk.

### Mandatory Perfection

Objects and situations with zero tolerance for error can be avoided. If standing on an exit that will severely punish a 180° or PC hesitation, the jumper must recognize the stakes and accept them consciously.

# Advanced Technique: Tracking

## Tracking

Three ingredients to efficient tracking:
- **Angle of Attack**
- **Surface Area**
- **Body Configuration**

### Angle of Attack

Most common error: tracking too flat (too high AoA). Start slightly steeper than intuitive and adjust while monitoring glide performance. Skydivers often track for hang time, not distance — in BASE, distance is critical.

Finding the correct AoA as soon as possible is key. A steep dive to "gain airspeed" is not optimal — the sooner you find the best angle for distance, the sooner you move forward. Rapid acceleration occurs in the correct position (still in freefall). Lean forward, push out from exit.

### Surface Area

More surface area = more lift potential (but only at the correct AoA — wrong AoA = only drag). Arms straight at sides (not too close, allowing suit inflation). Legs just wide enough for full suit inflation. Hands behind the butt does not create a lifting effect — it reduces total surface area.

### Body Configuration

Slightly curved, de-arched configuration:
- De-arched evenly from pointed toes to slightly cupped shoulders (gentle even arc from side view)
- Upper back lifting sensation indicates correct position
- Chin tucked to chest (minimal airstream interference)
- Arms at sides, wide enough for surface area but not excessive drag
- Legs wide enough for full pant inflation, not wider
- Elongated as much as possible without excessive tension
- All of the above at the correct AoA

## Sub-Terminal Tracking

Sub-terminal tracking (STT) is mandatory for most slider-up objects. Same rules as terminal tracking, plus body balance on exit.

From 0 to ~5 seconds, there is little wind resistance — most hinges on exit push and immediate body position. A weak/head-high box-man position produces poor results. A powerful forward push and immediate tracking position produce good separation by 4-6 seconds. The difference at sub-terminal walls: a 180° can be <1 second from the cliff, or 3 seconds away.

**Technique:** Meld exit with track — they should be one. Push forward, leap, achieve tracking position within 2 seconds. Run off the exit if possible. Each second of track gains more distance (track furthest in the last second). Start the track before leaving the object.

## Deployment Altitude and Object Separation

The safest pull altitude is not always the highest. If malfunction will be an off-heading (the most common scenario), extra separation from the cliff can be more valuable than extra altitude above ground.

Example: 1600' cliff, sheer for 800', then positive. Pull at 1000' with a 180° = ~150' separation. Pull at 1250' = 200'+ separation. Extra separation can mean the difference between kicking out line twists safely and wall impact.

Track as far away as possible and pull as far from the object as possible. The greatest tracking distance is gained during the last second of freefall.

## Video as Risk Factor

Video changes the jump. A camera is both a physical snag hazard and a psychological distraction/motivator. Video can motivate jumpers to make something "look good" or try something new. The prospect of peer adulation creates a dangerous dynamic. Ego-driven motivation to impress is dangerous and often unconscious.

To minimize video-related risk: turn camera on well before exit, forget about it by jump time, focus on the jump alone, remember the camera only after landing.

## Aerials

Aerials exponentially increase danger. Even a simple gainer requires careful planning, instruction, supervision, and a forgiving object. Extensive training is mandatory.

**Risks:**
- **Bridle entanglements:** Equipment designed for belly-to-earth deployment. Deploying from head-up, head-down, or back-to-earth positions risks bridle wrapping around arms, legs, or torso.
- **Pin lock:** Bridle pulling at an angle that traps the pin on many container systems.
- **Loss of altitude awareness:** Spinning horizon is disorienting. High-dive training necessary.
- **Instability:** Inherently unstable positions are bad for deployments.
- **Off-headings and object strike.**

**Gainer deployment timing:** Initiate deployment in head-down position so canopy extraction begins in belly-to-earth.

**Front somersaults:** Smaller window of opportunity — not advisable to initiate deployment from head-up or seated position.

**Back-to-earth exits:** Loss of altitude awareness is a known serious failure mode.

# Advanced Technique: Wingsuit BASE

## Progression Requirements

Before first wingsuit BASE jump:
- At least 100 wingsuit skydives (including 40-60 with the specific suit to be used)
- At least 100 regular BASE jumps with a long record of perfect exits
- Exits blending seamlessly into tracks
- BASE openings perfect 95% of the time
- Comfortable with sub-terminal tracking from slider-up cliffs
- Calm and relaxed on exit point (nervousness is healthy; paralyzing fear indicates unreadiness)

Wingsuit skydiving experience needed:
- Full speed range from stall to dive
- Highly competent at regaining stability from any configuration
- Hundreds of practice pulls in all body positions
- Riser control mastered
- Aggressive, controlled PC deployment technique

Continued skydiving is essential for progression — the best WS BASE jumpers skydive regularly.

## Equipment Selection

Begin with a beginner-intermediate wingsuit with good range, decent performance, easy movement on ground and in air, rapid riser control, and easy BOC access. Do not start with a large/high-performance suit.

Even an entry-level suit requires 30-80 BASE jumps to reach full potential. Only then consider upgrading.

## Wingsuit Exits

**Common mistakes:**
- **Too head high:** Causes poor start as jumper pivots past correct AoA. Antidote: plenty of terminal exit experience without wingsuit.
- **Too head low:** From over-enthusiastic dive, looking too low, or unsteady footing. Exit should be steep enough to gain speed for flight but not so steep as to delay flight start.
- **Flying too slowly:** Desire to minimize sink rate forces too-high AoA, near/in stall. Ruins glide performance, sacrifices control, range, stability. Creates large burble detrimental to deployment.
- **Wobbling:** Pitch/roll instability from flying too slowly, being too tense, or aggressive de-arching.

## The Wingsuit Burble

Wingsuits magnify wake turbulence. The slower and less efficiently a wingsuit flies, the larger the burble. An efficient flight with reasonably low (steep) AoA increases laminar airflow over the back, reducing burble and PC hesitation chance. Pulling from slow flight or stall means PC hesitation — dangerous at BASE altitudes.

## Speed and Available Lift (AL)

**Available Lift (AL):** The potential lift available from the wingsuit if maximum lift were instantly demanded at any point in flight. AL is fluid and fluctuates with each flight phase.

**Flying at sustained max AL is not recommended** — it bleeds energy, stalls the wing, and kills. Flying WITH high AL in reserves is smart.

### AL and Airspeed Relationship

Force is proportional to velocity squared (F ∝ V²). This applies to both lift and drag.

| Speed Change | New AL |
|---|---|
| +10% | 121% |
| +20% | 144% |
| +50% | 225% |
| -10% | 81% (19% lost) |
| -20% | 64% (36% lost) |
| -50% | 25% (75% lost) |

Every extra increment of airspeed yields increasing returns in AL. Every speed decrease taps the dangerous part of the equation — speed is easy to lose, hard to regain (requires cashing in altitude).

Every wingsuit pilot can decrease airspeed 30% in seconds without trying — that is a 50% decrease in AL. This is why slow-speed flares are impossible: AL is exhausted.

### Why Slow Flight Kills

The common failure pattern: exit into slow flight, fly chest-high/head-high just above stall, try to hang in the air as long as possible. Suit stalls, drag increases. Pilot pitches up more, stalls deeper, sink rate builds. Emergency pitch leads to line stretch and impact.

### Three Reasons Pilots Fly Too Slowly

1. **Skydiving muscle memory:** Belly flying habits (head high, chest high, knees bent, hips arched) reinforced by flocking. Flocking trains slow, detuned flight — worst training for WS BASE.
2. **Flying fast is a difficult learned skill:** Requires deliberate training with significant flight time in speed-drill body position.
3. **Muscle fatigue:** Higher AL = higher forces on arm/shoulder muscles. Fatigue leads to relaxing arms, spilling air and lift — and weak PC deployments.

## Dihedral, Wing Sweep, and Configuration

**Dihedral:** Raising wingtips above wing roots (wrists higher than shoulders). Provides roll stability. Also good for pitch stability in dives. Creates drag — kills speed.

**Anhedral (negative dihedral):** Minimal lateral stability, impressive roll agility. Used by supersonic fighters and swifts.

**Wing Sweep:** Angle of arm's leading edge relative to shoulder axis. Aft wing sweep is a distortion-induced, lift-dumping method to steepen flight path. Burns altitude but can be converted to a flare. Induces fabric-billowing drag that hurts horizontal speed/distance.

**Distortion vs. Stability vs. Performance:** Any intentional distortion of the wingsuit (dihedral, extra aft sweep, dropped knees, loose grippers) generates drag. Stability requires isolated regions of drag acting as micro-drogues. More stability generally requires more drag. Lower drag = higher speed, lower stability. This is the fundamental aerodynamic compromise.

## Angle of Attack

AoA is the angle of the body's flat plane relative to direction of flight (relative to air mass). AoA is NOT flight angle and NOT glide ratio.

- Lower AoA → higher airspeed
- Higher AoA → lower airspeed
- Pilots must actively manage AoA — no wingsuit auto-settles to optimal

AoA and wingsuit configuration are independent. The same configuration held through different AoAs yields different results. AoA is the major performance factor; configuration refinements optimize performance at each angle.

**Center of gravity vs. center of pressure:** Many wingsuits have CoG behind CoP, creating natural tendency to fly head-high (too flat, too slowly). Flying head-high/flat is a widely recognized error. Conscious effort is required for steeper AoA.

## Wingsuit Configuration (formerly "Body Position")

The efficient configuration for speed:
- Gently de-arched from head to toe
- Only face and tip-toes touch imaginary ground (lying face down)
- Legs straight, arms strong and level, toes pointed for lengthwise tension
- Shoulders shrugged forward, slight concavity in front torso
- Forearms pushed into leading edge of arm wings
- Smooth surfaces encouraging laminar airflow top and bottom

Disrupting airflow (bending knees, distorting arm wings) reduces the laminar flow that creates lift.

To change speed/flare: change AoA (push toes down), not configuration. Never initiate a flare by bending knees — that is air-braking.

## Polar Curve and Speed Zones

The wingsuit polar curve shows the relationship between glide, speed, and sink rate:

- **Stall speed → Min sink speed → Best glide (MID) speed → Fast flight**
- **Red Zone:** Near-stall, low vertical speed, poor glide. Slow speed near-stall flight kills wingsuit pilots in BASE.
- **Orange Zone:** Best glide / MID speed. Not the healthiest speed — relatively close to stall, zero energy reserves. Do not habitually sacrifice speed for glide.
- **Green Zone:** Above MID speed. Horizontal speed increases with sink rate. Ideal range for both skydiving and BASE — allows converting retained energy to increase glide or disconnect from terrain.

**MID Speed (Maximum Impact Distance Speed):** The speed yielding maximum glide ratio. Flying faster or slower than MID results in shorter impact distance. However, flying significantly faster than MID over suitable terrain provides increased AL and maneuvering capability. Flying at or below MID provides no margin. MID Speed is a middle point, not a target speed for normal flight.

**Terminal velocity in wingsuit context:** Total speed (magnitude and direction) in whatever configuration/orientation, not just vertical. At 1:1 glide (45° flight path): 71% of terminal velocity is both vertical and horizontal. At 3:1 glide (18° path): 31% vertical, 95% horizontal. These proportions indicate high drag and low lift.

## Wingsuit Openings

### Deployment Process

The PC deployment is the beginning of a process requiring full active participation. Common mistake: stop flying once BOC reach begins. Fly the body and suit symmetrically through the entire deployment:

- Maintain heading toward a horizon marker
- Keep shoulders level and perpendicular to line of flight
- Once risers load, keep load equal by reacting to feedback
- Deploy PC with clear, unwavering intent to get it into clean air well to the side
- Reach back symmetrically (most pilots use both hands)

### Airspeed for Deployment

Not too fast (equipment damage, violent openings), not too slow (PC in burble). Process: fast/normal flight → brief flare/slow-down → level off until clean air flows over top surface → deploy symmetrically.

### Two Deployment Transition Techniques

**1. Intentional Flare:**
- Best flare emerges from level flight, not steep dive (level out first, then go head-high)
- As climbing, higher AoA needed to continue (most head-high at apex)
- Deploying at apex: large burble but low airspeed (manageable with correct PC/bridle)
- Do not deploy after apex — risk head-low deployment causing twists

**2. Relax/Reduce:**
- Relax body configuration, de-power suit, reduce speed
- Deploy at more level configuration, moderately reduced speed
- More airflow over top surface than cobra-position deployment

### Full Flight Deployment

Most advanced pilots do not collapse wings. Key factors: AoA control, airspeed control, reach technique.

**Reach technique:** Reach around top surface of arm wing, not through it. Squeeze as little air as possible from wings. Practice on ground with blower and hundreds of practice touches during skydives. BOC reach to PC release should take ~1 second.

**Critical:** Skydive BOC and BASE BOC are NEVER in the same place. Applying the same motion to a BASE rig that works on a skydive rig can result in missing the PC.

### Line Twists vs. Body Twists

**Line twists:** Occur behind the jumper during extraction. Result of packing and deployment asymmetries. Prevented by clean packing and wing symmetry at deployment, and stable PC.

**Body twists:** Occur at the body — jumper rotating beneath inflating canopy. Caused by deploying in a slight turn, loading inside riser with a dipped shoulder. Hard or violent openings aggravate body twists.

**Prevention:** Riser control. The moment the parachute loads, hands on risers. Hold risers apart to stop body rotation. In a wingsuit: deploy PC → immediately punch fists straight forward → reach up and grab risers.

**Untwisting solutions (in order):**
1. Manually un-twist: rotate twists from lines down to risers
2. Extend one arm wing into airflow as windmill
3. Kick

**Do not unzip leg wing while in twists** — it trails like a weather vane, making it very hard to rotate. Keep leg wing zipped until under a good parachute.

### Canopy Choice for Wingsuit

0-3cfm (F-111) material yields better, more consistent openings than ZP. Docile, 7-cell, low aspect ratio design is mandatory. Lightly loaded, larger than you normally would. Canopies smaller than ~120 sqft may have line sets short enough for the canopy to be sucked into the burble during deployment.

### Pull High

All parachutes can and will eventually malfunction. Line twists are practically unavoidable with wingsuits. Give yourself time and altitude to deal with problems.

## Terrain Flying

### Choosing Your Line

Common misconception: flying along the side of a cliff is safer than flying over something. Wingsuits do not turn as efficiently as believed — even pilots using steep bank angles drift and lose altitude. Beginners drift much more. Small lateral adjustments are far more technical than small vertical adjustments. The simplest trajectory is a straight line. A beginner putting themselves into a position requiring a turn away from the cliff is reckless.

### Understanding Margin

Margin in wingsuit terrain flying comes from **retained energy** (speed).

Analogy: Imagine a rubber band fixed 500' above a mountain slope at best-glide altitude. The more you stretch it by diving below best glide (closer to terrain, steeper, faster), the more margin you have — because you can convert speed to glide at any moment, separating from terrain.

The closer you are to steep terrain while diving, the more the rubber band is stretched. Common sense: closer to terrain = less reaction time. But the energy retained while diving steep creates the ability to escape.

**Key principle:** If you are straining to fly far and flat, you have almost no margin. If you are straining to fly steep enough to reach the terrain, you have plenty of margin retained. If you cannot convert a dive into a nice flare while skydiving, you are not ready to BASE jump near terrain.

Two ingredients to margin: (1) retained energy (speed), (2) distance from terrain.

### Flying Over Terrain

Major difference between flying **down to something** (margin-building dive) and putting yourself **behind something** (committed with no margin). Never put yourself in a situation where you "need" to increase glide to avoid an obstacle. Speed is your margin — keep it in your pocket.

Guidelines:
1. Choose steep and smooth terrain
2. Know your line intimately
3. Fly only in perfect meteorological conditions
4. Keep distance on first flight; fly closer gradually over several flights

### Flying Next to Terrain

Not inherently safer or more dangerous than flying over steep terrain with proper technique. Remember:
1. You will drift in any turn
2. You will lose altitude in any turn
3. Keep distance on first flight

### Flying Between Terrain

Greatly reduces available solutions. Depending on depth/width, may be impossible to use retained energy for vertical separation. Emergency pull likely means object strike. Only with full realization of lack of outs and total control.

### Training and Progression

Flying well and flying safely near terrain are different things. Until you understand actual drift in turns, actual altitude loss in turns, and actual suit stability in dives, you are not ready — even if you think you are. Progress slowly in steep, forgiving terrain over many sessions. A two-week vacation is not enough.

Upgrading wingsuits before fully utilizing current suit performance is a recognized error. A bigger wingsuit will not make you a better pilot.

### Wingsuit Start Arc

The start arc is the altitude lost at the moment the glide path intersects a 45° descending line from the exit point. A 500' start arc means 500' below the summit and 500' from the cliff.

Start arc varies with conditions (tail/headwind, lift/sink), exit technique, wing loading, and suit efficiency. Every exit is different — leave margin for conditions and performance variations.

### Effects of Variables on Start Arc

Variables have measurable effects on outcomes:
- **Wing loading:** Adding 25 lbs can change a successful start to terrain impact
- **Exit push strength:** Weak push significantly degrades start performance
- **Conditions:** 5 mph anabatic flow (lift) vs zero wind vs headwind can each shift the arc by tens of feet
- A wingsuit start is a controlled recovery from stall

### No-Pull Zones (NPZs)

Sections of flight where deployment is not survivable. Some start at exit; others appear 3-4 seconds in and last up to 8 seconds. Understanding how to predict, recognize, and mentally commit to flying through an NPZ at slow airspeed is essential.

### Conditions and Flight Planning

- Wind conditions along the entire flight must be anticipated, not just those felt at the exit
- A below-average start combined with stronger-than-anticipated headwinds can be fatal
- If conditions differ enough from normal, Plan B may not be survivable
- The 90% perfect conditions rule: conditions should be 90% perfect for 100% of the day. If not, do not hike up.
- South/east faces heat first — more thermal activity in morning
- Wind >10-15 km/h forecasted for exit altitude is generally a bad sign

## Safety Guidelines for Wingsuit Flight Near Terrain

Whenever below safe opening altitude:

**Before exiting:**
- Physical warm-up (essential for performance flying)
- Knowledge of area: landmarks, hazards (power lines)
- Reconnaissance: on foot, paraglider, aircraft. Evaluate landings, hazards, technical sections
- Plan B and Plan C with team briefing
- See (or know intimately) entire line from exit to landing. If you cannot, do not exit

**During flight, mandatory YES to:**
- Do I have enough speed?
- Do I have enough glide?
- Do I have an escape route?

**A "maybe" is a NO.** A NO means immediate deployment. It is always better to deploy late than never.

# Staying Alive

## Read Your Own Incident Report

Before jumping, analyze decisions from an outside perspective. If the description of preparations would sound like the opening of an accident report ("The jumper exited in high winds..."), reconsider. Comparative optimism (believing oneself less likely to suffer misfortune than others) affects all jumpers.

## Patience and Progression

Every day of quality BASE knowledge and practice builds both skill and judgment. Patient progression develops the muscle memory needed for serious situations. **Do not expose yourself to the possibility of needing a skill you have not practiced thoroughly.**

Moving to advanced objects without experiencing necessary survival skills is endangering your life. Practicing off-heading corrections and line twist recovery extensively at a span means that when it happens on a cliff (it will), the reaction is natural and instant.

## Go Long, Not Hard

Jumpers who make a habit of consistent, low-key jumping are often capable of impressive stunts but put themselves in those situations rarely. They recognize that the only way to mitigate risk is to reduce exposure — consistently jumping below capability.

Complacency is widely regarded as dangerous. No jump should be assumed routine. Maintaining attention to detail on every jump is a core community norm.

## Fatal Decisions Are Made Early

Fatal decisions occur before the exit point — when waking up and deciding to go despite marginal weather, arriving at an unfamiliar jump late enough to fly in the dark, or not setting clear personal limits.

Setting personal rules (e.g., no aerials from sub-terminal objects, no night jumping at unfamiliar sites) removes the need to debate decisions under pressure at the exit point. Pre-committing to limits is an accepted practice.

When analyzing fatality circumstances, individual factors often appear justifiable in isolation ("jumped at night many times before," "jumped in wind many times before"). The cumulative risk is rarely assessed before exiting. Recognizing stacked risk factors is critical.

Hiking in marginal conditions commits the jumper psychologically. The decision not to jump is easiest before approaching the object. The safest action is to not start.

## Self-Evaluation

Accurately evaluating personal skill is essential. A widespread issue in wingsuit BASE is the misconception about personal ability — jumpers believe they are better than they are. Putting on a wingsuit and jumping from a cliff does not make one "experienced."

Without classifications or skill levels (as in whitewater kayaking or rock climbing), jumpers routinely misclassify themselves. It is possible to fly close to terrain without much skill and survive a few times, creating false confidence.

## Fear as Safety Signal

Fear should keep jumpers in check when they are not protecting themselves adequately. Blocking out fear is unhealthy. Accepting fear allows working through anxiety.

At the exit point: analyze whether fear comes from a rational place (complexity, weather, inadequate skill, no contingency plan). Rational fear = walk down. Irrational fear, once identified as meritless, tends to dissipate.

## Encouraging Unsafe Behavior

Unsafe behavior is encouraged subtly: bragging about rapid progression, talking down on progression norms, lending confidence ("you'll be fine"), facilitating jumps instead of teaching correct execution, describing new skills as "easy" rather than identifying risks, laughing off mistakes. Casual dismissal of incidents has far-reaching negative impact on how others perceive risk.

## Judgment Over Skill

An excellent jumper uses excellent judgment to avoid needing excellent skill. Motivations that prevent safe jumping: desire for group acceptance, believing oneself above recommended progression, desire to be seen as equal to admired jumpers, borrowing confidence to push beyond boundaries.

## Mentorship and Progression

A first jump course does not make a complete BASE jumper. New jumpers should check their ego, use better judgment, and not rush the progression.

## Female Progression Considerations

Inexperienced female jumpers are at risk of being fast-tracked to objects and progression stages they don't belong at, often driven by attraction dynamics in mentor/student relationships. Awareness of this phenomenon is the first step. Choose mentors with whom the relationship is platonic. Truly platonic relationships are rare; even one-way feelings may increase risk exposure.

**Warning signs:** "You'll be fine"; mentors showing romantic interest; partying before jumping.

## Currency

Being current sharpens reaction times and skills but also builds confidence that can become complacency. Current jumpers must maintain the beginner's mind: openness to stepping back and recognizing how much remains to be learned.

# GLOSSARY

**Chord:** The distance between leading edge and trailing edge at the center of the wing.

**Span:** The distance between each wingtip, across the length of the wing.

**Aspect Ratio:** The ratio of span to chord length (AR = s² / A = s² / (s × c) = s / c).

**Wing loading:** The ratio of jumper weight (including all equipment) to wing surface area, expressed in lbs per square foot.

**Leading Edge:** The front, or 'nose' side of the wing.

**Trailing Edge:** The rear of the wing, to which the control lines are attached.

**Top Surface:** The top 'skin' of a wing.

**Bottom Surface:** The bottom 'skin' of a wing.

**Cell:** A chordwise section of a ram-air canopy. In skydiving terms, a canopy with 14 countable cells has 7 cells (unsupported ribs are dividers between halves of a single cell).

**Rib:** The vertical section of fabric connecting top and bottom surfaces.

**Load Tape:** Load-bearing webbing reinforcing key parts of the canopy including leading edge, ribs, and line attachment points.

**Panel:** Any single side of a cell on the top or bottom surface.

**Stabilizer:** The wingtip section — typically a small single-surface section pulled to near-vertical by stabilizer lines. Runs perpendicular to the horizontal plane of the canopy.

**0-3 CFM (F-111):** Material spec for BASE canopies and reserve parachutes. Up to 3 cubic feet of air can pass through one square foot of cloth per minute under 1/2 inch of water pressure. A porosity gauge — ZP (zero porosity) cloth has zero porosity when new. Both increase porosity with use.

**Angle of Attack (AoA):** The acute angle between the chord of an airfoil and the undisturbed relative airflow.

**Sink Rate:** The rate of vertical descent, usually expressed in feet per minute or meters per second.

**Glide Ratio:** The ratio of horizontal to vertical distance.

**Airspeed:** Velocity relative to the surrounding air mass.

**Groundspeed:** Speed relative to the ground.

**Control Lines (Brake Lines):** Lines connecting the trailing edge of the canopy to the brake toggles.

**Spin:** Yaw-axis rotation caused by asymmetric stall — one side stalls and flies backward, rotating around the flying side. Sensation: rotating office chair with no extra G-loading. Difficult to maintain for more than a few rotations.

**Spiral:** Roll/yaw-axis rotation with increased speed and G-force. Neither side of the wing is stalled. Malfunction-induced spirals may continue indefinitely if uncorrected. Pilot-induced spirals self-correct when input ceases.

**Articulation:** In BASE harness manufacture, a ring system joining main webbing structures at the hip, allowing greater freedom of movement.

**MLW (Main Lift Web):** The section of harness webbing bearing the majority of the jumper's load, running from riser attachment point to leg straps.

**Shrivel Flap:** The soft (usually Velcro) section positioned on the bridle, used to close the main container opening.

**Terminal Object:** An object with enough vertical relief for a BASE jumper to reach terminal velocity in freefall.

**Usable Altitude:** The altitude available for freefall and then landing in the planned landing area — not the total height of the object.

**DBS (Deep Brake Setting):** The brake setting used for slider-down openings, configured so the canopy flies as close to stall as safely possible.

**SBS (Shallow Brake Setting):** The brake setting used for slider-up openings, equivalent to the standard skydiving brake setting.

**LRM (Line Release Modification):** Routing brake lines outside guide rings and slider grommets for slider-down jumps, allowing toggle release to clear line-over malfunctions.

**PCA (Pilot Chute Assist):** A deployment method where an assistant holds the PC/bridle and releases at line stretch as the jumper exits.

**BOC (Bottom of Container):** The location of the pilot chute pocket on the container system.

**NPZ (No-Pull Zone):** A section of wingsuit flight where deployment is not survivable due to terrain proximity.

**MID Speed (Maximum Impact Distance Speed):** The airspeed yielding maximum glide ratio. Flying faster or slower results in shorter impact distance.

**AL (Available Lift):** The potential lift available from a wingsuit if maximum lift were instantly demanded at any point in flight.

**Start Arc:** The altitude lost at the moment a wingsuit pilot's glide path intersects a 45° descending line from the exit point.

**Death Camper:** Informal term for someone who begins BASE with zero skydive experience.

**Whuffo:** Semi-derogatory term for persons uninitiated to skydiving/BASE jumping.
